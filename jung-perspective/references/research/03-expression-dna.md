# 03 - Jung's Expression DNA: Writing Style and Linguistic Fingerprint

> A comprehensive analysis of Carl Gustav Jung's expression style, linguistic habits, and rhetorical DNA across his entire corpus -- from the early scientific papers to the visionary *Red Book*, from the academic *Collected Works* to the intimate late autobiography *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*.

---

## 1. SENTENCE ARCHITECTURE: The German Syntax Burden

### Long, Subordinate-Rich Sentences

Jung typically writes in **long, multi-clause sentences** with nested subordination -- a characteristic that reflects both his German-native syntax and his intellectual ambition to hold multiple ideas simultaneously. This is most pronounced in the *Collected Works*:

> "Just as the causal principle, which rules the world of natural science, is not in a position to explain the workings of telepathy, so the final principle, which governs the world of the spirit, is no less in a position to explain the facts of psychopathology, which to all appearances belong to a mechanistic type of connection." -- *CW 8, "Synchronicity: An Acausal Connecting Principle"*

Key structural features:
- **Cumulative clauses**: Jung rarely writes a simple subject-verb-object sentence. He elaborates, then elaborates on the elaboration.
- **The colon as pivot**: He frequently uses colons to introduce definitions or amplifications mid-sentence -- a signature move.
- **Periodic structure**: The main verb often arrives late, with qualifying clauses front-loaded.
- **Germanic hypotaxis**: In his German originals, subordinate clauses are stacked; even in English translations this architecture is preserved.

### Variation Across Genres

This tendency varies dramatically by context:

| Genre | Sentence Profile | Example Texts |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| Early academic papers (1902-1912) | Dense, cautious, heavily subordinated, Latinate vocabulary | *On the Psychology and Pathology of So-Called Occult Phenomena*, *Studies in Word Association* |
| *Collected Works* essays (1916-1951) | Long compound-complex sentences, definition-heavy, circling | *Psychological Types*, *Aion*, *Psychology and Alchemy* |
| Seminars & lectures (1925-1940s) | Shorter, conversational, anecdote-filled, emphatic repetitions | *Tavistock Lectures*, *Zarathustra Seminars*, *The Visions Seminars* |
| *The Red Book* (1914-1930) | Fragmented, prophetic, oracular short lines mixed with narrative passages | *Liber Novus* |
| Late popular work (1957-1961) | Shortest average sentence length, carefully edited for general reader, direct address | *Man and His Symbols*, *The Undiscovered Self* |

**Source**: Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (Routledge, 2005); Mathew V. Spano, "Modern(-ist) Man in Search of a Soul" (*Jung Page*, 2012).

## 2. ANALOGY DENSITY AND METAPHOR USAGE

### High Analogical Density

Jung's writing is **analogy-rich** -- perhaps among the most analogically dense in the entire Western psychological canon. He reasons *through* analogy rather than using it as ornamentation. This is not stylistic excess but a methodological commitment: for Jung, analogy is the *epistemological bridge* between the known and the unknown, the conscious and the unconscious.

Common analogy domains:
1. **Alchemy** -- the *opus* as model for individuation; the *lapis* as symbol of the Self; *coniunctio* as union of opposites
2. **Geology/stratification** -- psyche as layered ("the personal unconscious rests upon a deeper layer, the collective unconscious, just as personal experience rests upon inherited brain structure")
3. **Water/ocean** -- the unconscious as sea, consciousness as island emerging from it
4. **Light/illumination** -- "making conscious" as bringing light into darkness
5. **Energy/electricity** -- libido as "psychic energy" modeled on physical energy
6. **Architecture** -- the psyche as a house with floors, the Tower at Bollingen as built symbol
7. **Botany/growth** -- individuation as the unfolding of an acorn into an oak tree (an Aristotelian/entelechy model)

### The "Aesthetic Science" Framework

Susan Rowland argues that Jung's writing constitutes an **"aesthetic science"** -- a hybrid that deliberately elides boundaries between art and science. The metaphors are not decorative. They *do the argumentative work*. Jung does not say "the unconscious is *like* an ocean"; he structures the entire argument so that the reader experiences the unconscious *as* oceanic.

**Source**: Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (Routledge, 2005); Rowland, "Jung, the Trickster Writer" (*Journal of Analytical Psychology*, 2006).

## 3. HIGH-FREQUENCY VOCABULARY AND PROPRIETARY TERMINOLOGY

### The Sacred Lexicon

Jung's writing is built around a core vocabulary of **proprietary terms** -- words he either invented or radically redefined. These function as structuring nodes around which his arguments orbit:

| Term | Jung's Definition | First Appearance |
|------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Complex** (Gefuehlsbetonter Komplex) | Emotionally-toned cluster of ideas | *Studies in Word Association* (1904-1909) |
| **Archetype** (Archetypus) | Inherited pattern of psychic functioning; "the tendency to form such representations of a motif" | *Instinct and the Unconscious* (1919); systematized in *The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious* (1934) |
| **Collective unconscious** (Kollektives Unbewusstes) | The deeper, impersonal layer of the psyche shared by all humanity | *The Structure of the Unconscious* (1916) |
| **Individuation** (Individuation) | The process of becoming a psychological individual; "the process by which a person becomes a psychological 'in-dividual,' that is, a separate, indivisible unity or whole" | *Psychological Types* (1921), Definitions section |
| **Persona** | The mask or social role one presents to the world | ibid. |
| **Shadow** (Schatten) | The personal unconscious, the "negative" side of the personality | *On the Psychology of the Unconscious* (1917) |
| **Anima/Animus** | The inner feminine in man / inner masculine in woman | *Psychological Types* (1921) |
| **Self** (Selbst) | The archetype of wholeness; the totality that transcends the ego | *The Relations Between the Ego and the Unconscious* (1928) |
| **Synchronicity** (Synchronizitaet) | "An acausal connecting principle" | *Synchronicity: An Acausal Connecting Principle* (1952); term first used publicly in 1930 in his memorial address for Richard Wilhelm |
| **Transcendent function** (Transzendente Funktion) | The psychic function that unites conscious and unconscious, producing a new, third position | *The Transcendent Function* (written 1916, published 1958) |
| **Enantiodromia** | The tendency of things to turn into their opposites; borrowed from Heraclitus | Throughout the *Collected Works* |
| **Psychoid** | The quasi-psychic, quasi-physical realm at the deepest level of archetypes | *On the Nature of the Psyche* (1947) |
| **Unus mundus** | "One world" -- the transcendental unity underlying psyche and matter | *Mysterium Coniunctionis* (1955); borrowed from alchemical philosophy |
| **Coniunctio** | The alchemical conjunction/sacred marriage, psychological union of opposites | *The Psychology of the Transference* (1946) |
| **Active imagination** (Aktive Imagination) | The method of consciously engaging unconscious contents through vision, dialogue, art | First fully described in *The Transcendent Function* (1916) |

### Sacred Incantatory Words Beyond Technical Terms

Beyond his formal terminology, Jung repeatedly returns to certain **value-laden words** that function almost as incantations:
- **"Numinous"** (from Rudolf Otto's *das Numinose*) -- used to describe experiences charged with sacred/archetypal energy; one of his most frequent non-technical adjectives
- **"Meaning"** (Sinn) -- the ultimate criterion for psychological validity; "The decisive question for man is: is he related to something infinite or not?"
- **"Whole"/"Wholeness"** (Ganzheit) -- the telos of individuation
- **"Soul"** (Seele) -- Jung deliberately uses this pre-scientific word where Freud would have said "psyche," marking his break with reductive materialism
- **"Dark"/"Darkness"** (Dunkel) -- a pervasive spatial metaphor for the unknown/unconscious

### The Absence of These Terms in *The Red Book*

A striking finding noted by editor Sonu Shamdasani: *The Red Book* contains **none** of Jung's later technical vocabulary. No "archetype," no "unconscious," no "individuation," no "shadow." It is, as Shamdasani puts it, **"Jung without his conceptual arsenal"** -- a "lyrical elaboration" where the figures (Philemon, Salome, Elijah) *embody* what later became theoretical constructs.

**Source**: Sonu Shamdasani, Introduction to *The Red Book: Liber Novus* (Norton, 2009); C.G. Jung, *Psychological Types* (1921), "Definitions" chapter; C.G. Jung, *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (1961).

## 4. THE UNCERTAINTY CONTINUUM: How Jung Expressed (and Did Not Express) Doubt

### The Striking Absence of "I Don't Know"

Jung **rarely says "I don't know"** in his writings. This is a significant observation about his rhetorical posture. When he confronts the limits of his understanding, he employs a range of strategies that maintain epistemic authority while acknowledging mystery:

### Jung's Uncertainty Repertoire

**Strategy 1: Redescription as "mystery" (Mysterium)**
When Jung is at the limit of explanation, he does not retreat into agnosticism. He *redescribes* the unknown as *mysterium* -- a sacred, numinous unknown that is itself the object of investigation.

> "The unconscious is not just unknown, it is *unknownable* in principle. And yet we must speak of it." -- *CW 9i, "Archetypes of the Collective Unconscious"*

**Strategy 2: Anticipatory concession**
Jung frequently concedes potential objections preemptively -- not with "I might be wrong" but with "the reader may object that..." or "one might be tempted to think..." -- absorbing skepticism into his argumentative frame without destabilizing his own position.

**Strategy 3: The polysemic "so to speak" (sozusagen)**
One of Jung's most frequent hedges is the German *sozusagen* (translated as "so to speak" or "as it were"). It functions as a distancing device: "These archetypes are, so to speak, the organs of the psyche." It signals that he is using metaphorical language without undermining the ontological claim.

**Strategy 4: The phrase "as experience shows" (wie die Erfahrung zeigt)**
Jung appeals to "experience" -- his own clinical practice and decades of observation -- as an evidential foundation. "Experience shows" operates as a truth-claim assertion that sidesteps the need for experimental verification or statistical proof.

**Strategy 5: The admission embedded in the assertion**
When Jung does approach admitting uncertainty, he embeds it inside an assertion that makes the admission itself evidence of intellectual seriousness:

> "I must confess that I have never encountered a case where the personal unconscious did not also contain collective contents." (CW 7)

The confession structure ("I must confess") paradoxically strengthens his authority: he is honest enough to admit what might seem to weaken his case, and the admission becomes evidence *for* the case.

**Strategy 6: The "I know" moment**
Perhaps the most famous example is the 1959 BBC *Face to Face* interview. Asked "Do you now believe in God?", Jung replied: *"I know. I don't need to believe. I know."* He later clarified: "I did not say, 'There is a God.' I said 'I do not need to believe in God; I know.' Which does not mean: I do know a certain God (Zeus, Yahwe, Allah...) but rather: I do know that I am obviously confronted with a factor unknown in itself, which I call 'God.'" This is the ultimate Jungian uncertainty move: asserting *gnosis* (direct inner knowing) while simultaneously disclaiming cognitive certainty about the *object* of that knowing.

**Source**: C.G. Jung, BBC *Face to Face* interview, October 22, 1959; Jung's letter to *The Listener* (January 1960); *CW 7, Two Essays on Analytical Psychology*; Sonu Shamdasani, *Jung and the Making of Modern Psychology* (Cambridge, 2003).

## 5. HUMOR: Dry, Earthy, Self-Deprecating

### Jung Had Humor, and It Mattered

Jung is neither a comedian nor a wit in the Oscar Wilde sense. His humor is **dry** (trocken), **earthy**, often **self-deprecating**, and deployed strategically to undercut pretension -- especially his own. Those who knew him attested that in person he laughed frequently, told earthy stories, and was not the solemn sage that the *Collected Works* might suggest.

### Key Examples

**The "sane man" quip** (reported by his assistant Jolande Jacobi):
> "Show me a sane man and I will cure him for you."

This is quintessential Jungian humor: paradoxical, deflationary, hiding a serious point. It undercuts psychiatric pretension (the doctor doesn't have it figured out either), normalizes human strangeness (nobody's fully "sane"), and is funny precisely because it reverses the expected relationship between doctor and patient.

**The "Jung vs. Jungian" self-deflation**:
> "Thank God I'm Jung and not a Jungian."

The pun on his own name is delightfully self-deprecating. It expresses his fear that his exploratory ideas would become crystallized into dogma, that followers would imitate him (pipes, tweed jackets, Swiss accent) rather than undertaking their own individuation. The joke is that being "Jung" (a flawed, searching individual) is far more authentic than being a card-carrying "Jungian" (a member of a tribe). As analyst Jason E. Smith notes: "The real problem would not so much be in wanting to become a Jungian, but in wanting to *be* Jung."

**To his wife** (reported by Jung's biographer Vincent Brome):
> "If I get another perfectly normal adult malingering as a sick patient, I'll have him certified!"

**To his American friend George Beckwith**:
> "I'm sometimes driven to the conclusion that boring people need treatment more urgently than mad people."

### Humor's Strategic Function

Jung's humor serves specific argumentative purposes:
1. **Deflation of psychiatric arrogance** -- the therapist is as strange as the patient
2. **Normalization of psychological suffering** -- "sane" is a fiction
3. **Resistance to dogmatization** -- don't make a church out of my ideas
4. **Making the esoteric accessible** -- laughter bridges the gap between the consulting room and the reader

### What Jung's Humor Is NOT

There is little evidence of:
- **Sarcasm** -- Jung's wit is warm, not cutting
- **Irony in the modern/postmodern sense** -- he doesn't typically use ironic detachment
- **Puns or wordplay for its own sake** -- the "Jung/Jungian" pun is exceptional
- **Satire** -- he does not mock individuals or institutions with sustained satirical intent

**Source**: Vincent Brome, *Jung: Man and Myth* (1978); Jason E. Smith, "Thank God I'm a Jungian: Reflections for Thanksgiving" (*Jungian Online*, 2015); quote from George Beckwith via Brome; "sane man" quip verified by Quote Investigator (2012).

## 6. CITATION AND QUOTATION HABITS: The Great Tapestry

### Most Frequently Cited Authors and Sources

Jung's writing is saturated with quotations. He constructs his arguments through a **polyphonic mosaic** of voices, weaving together a vast range of sources from radically different epochs and traditions. The frequency order, based on cross-referencing his indexes and scholarly analysis, is roughly:

**Tier 1: Most Frequent**
- **Goethe** -- Jung's intellectual north star. *Faust* (especially Part II) is quoted/referenced more than any other literary work. Goethe is treated as the paradigmatic "whole man" who achieved individuation.
- **The Bible** -- Old and New Testament. The Book of Job in particular becomes the centerpiece of *Answer to Job* (1952), his most theologically provocative late work.
- **Nietzsche** -- *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* is the most frequently cited, but Jung also draws heavily on *Beyond Good and Evil* and *The Gay Science*. Jung's relationship to Nietzsche is deeply ambivalent: he saw Nietzsche as a cautionary tale of inflation by the unconscious.
- **Alchemical texts** -- A vast library: *Rosarium Philosophorum*, *Aurora Consurgens*, *Turba Philosophorum*, the works of Paracelsus, Michael Maier, Gerhard Dorn, and the *Corpus Hermeticum* attributed to Hermes Trismegistus. Jung dedicated his late years (c. 1935-1955) to the systematic study of alchemical literature, treating it as a symbolic map of the individuation process.

**Tier 2: Very Frequent**
- **Gnostic texts** -- especially the *Corpus Hermeticum*, the Nag Hammadi library texts, the writings of Basilides and Valentinus (often through secondary sources by Hippolytus and Irenaeus). Jung saw Gnosticism as the historical precursor of his own psychology.
- **Early Church Fathers** -- Augustine (especially on the Trinity and grace), Origen, Tertullian (famous for "credo quia absurdum").
- **Kant** -- the epistemological foundation for Jung's distinction between the phenomenal and the noumenal; Kant's *Critique of Pure Reason* is the philosophical backbone of Jung's claim that we can never know "things in themselves" (including the archetypes-as-such).
- **Schopenhauer** -- the will as blind striving, the influence on the concept of the unconscious.
- **Schiller** -- especially the *Letters on the Aesthetic Education of Man*, which Jung analyzed extensively in *Psychological Types* (1921).

**Tier 3: Frequent**
- **Plato / Neoplatonism** -- the theory of Ideas as precursor to archetypes; Plotinus on the One
- **The *I Ching*** (Book of Changes) -- Jung's foreword to the Wilhelm/Baynes translation (1949) is one of his most personal and speculative short texts. He used the *I Ching* personally and incorporated its principle of "meaningful coincidence" into the concept of synchronicity.
- **Meister Eckhart** -- the medieval German mystic whose language of the *Godhead* beyond God resonated with Jung's concept of the Self
- **Dante** -- *The Divine Comedy* as a map of the soul's journey
- **The Upanishads** and **The Tibetan Book of the Dead** -- Jung wrote psychological commentaries on both
- **Jakob Boehme** -- the German mystic whose paradoxes and visions Jung saw as spontaneous products of the unconscious
- **Laozi / Daoism** -- Jung's late-life engagement with Chinese thought; the *Dao De Jing* and *The Secret of the Golden Flower* (which he published with a psychological commentary in 1929)

### Quotation Style

- Jung frequently quotes **at length**, embedding large blocks of text rather than brief allusions. He expects the reader to sit with the quoted passage.
- He quotes **to authenticate, not merely to illustrate** -- a citation is not decoration but evidence that a psychic pattern has manifested independently across centuries. The alchemist's description of the *lapis* is *evidence* for the archetype of the Self, not a mere "comparison."
- He has a distinctive habit of **quoting untranslated Latin, Greek, and German** -- a practice that has made him notoriously difficult for general readers. The *Collected Works* translators often left technical terms in the original.
- His **footnote density is extreme** in the academic works. Some pages of *Mysterium Coniunctionis* have more footnote text than body text. The footnotes frequently spiral into mini-essays.

**Source**: C.G. Jung, *Collected Works* (20 volumes, 1953-1979), General Index volumes; *Answer to Job* (1952); *Psychological Types* (1921); Jung's foreword to the *I Ching* (1949); *Psychology and Alchemy* (1944); *Mysterium Coniunctionis* (1955).

## 7. PARADOX AND CONTRADICTION: Not Resolved, But Held

### The Signature Move: Sustaining Rather Than Resolving Contradiction

Jung's entire psychology is built on paradox, and his prose *enacts* this commitment. He does not treat contradiction as a bug to be eliminated through logic but as a feature of psychic reality that must be **held in conscious tension**. This is perhaps the single most defining feature of his expression DNA.

### The Transcendent Function as Linguistic Operation

Jung described the "transcendent function" as the psychic process that unites conscious and unconscious contents, thesis and antithesis, to produce a new, uniting symbol that could not have been logically predicted. His writing *performs* this function:

> "The ego keeps its integrity only if it does not identify with one of the opposites, and if it understands how to hold the balance between them. This is possible only if it remains conscious of both at once." -- *CW 8*

### Key Paradoxical Structures in Jung's Prose

**1. The "on the one hand... on the other hand" pattern**
Jung rarely takes a one-sided position. His characteristic move is to state both sides of a contradiction and then hold them, without synthesizing:

> "We must be able to let things happen in the psyche. For us, this is an art of which most people know nothing. Consciousness is forever interfering, helping, correcting, and negating, and never leaving the simple growth of the psychic processes in peace." -- *The Secret of the Golden Flower*, Commentary

**2. The contradiction is the point**
Jung treats contradiction not as error but as evidence of depth:

> "The self is absolutely paradoxical in that it represents in every respect thesis and antithesis, and at the same time synthesis." -- *CW 12, Psychology and Alchemy*

**3. The Axiom of Maria as structural frame**
Jung repeatedly returns to the alchemical Axiom of Maria: *"One becomes two, two becomes three, and out of the third comes the One as the Fourth."* This four-stage paradox becomes the structural pattern of his own argumentation: differentiation (one into two), conscious opposition (two clash), transcendent symbol (the third), new wholeness (One as the Fourth).

**4. The non-Hegelian dialectic**
Crucially, Jung's dialectic is **not** Hegelian thesis-antithesis-synthesis. He explicitly rejected both the Aristotelian golden mean (compromise) and Hegelian dialectic (mechanical cycle). His alternative is a *tertium non datur* -- "a third not given" -- a genuinely unpredictable emergence from the unconscious that transcends the initial opposition without reducing or compromising either pole.

### The "Coincidentia Oppositorum" Vocabulary

Jung draws on Nicholas of Cusa's concept of *coincidentia oppositorum* (the coincidence of opposites) throughout his mature work, along with the alchemical terms *coniunctio* (conjunction/sacred marriage) and *complexio* (interweaving). These become not just concepts but **linguistic rituals** -- repeated, ritualized returns to the same paradox until the reader begins to experience, rather than merely understand, the non-dual structure of psychic reality.

### The Answer to Job: Paradox as Polemic

Jung's most extreme enactment of this is *Answer to Job* (1952), in which he treats the biblical deity as a character whose psychology can be analyzed. The book's argument is itself paradoxical: God is unconscious, God evolves through human consciousness, Yahweh's injustice to Job represents a divine moral failing that the Incarnation (Christ) partly -- but not fully -- repairs through the *assumptio Mariae* (the Assumption of Mary as the fourth, feminine element of the Trinity). In no other text does Jung push the paradox of the God-image further, or with more passionate, almost angry, rhetorical energy.

> "Yahweh is not split but is an antinomy -- a totality of inner opposites -- and this is the indispensable condition for his tremendous dynamism, his omniscience and omnipotence." -- *Answer to Job*, CW 11

**Source**: C.G. Jung, *CW 7, Two Essays*; *CW 8, The Structure and Dynamics of the Psyche*; *CW 12, Psychology and Alchemy*; *CW 11, Answer to Job* (1952); Mills, J., "Jung on Transcendence" (Duquesne University / IAJS); Sanford L. Drob, *Archetype of the Absolute: The Unity of Opposites in Mysticism, Philosophy, and Psychology* (Fielding University Press).

## 8. THE POETIC-SCHOLARLY SPECTRUM: Where Jung Falls

### Not a Poet, Not a Pure Scientist -- A Third Thing

Jung is neither a poet nor a systematic scientist in the conventional sense. His writing operates in a **genre-between-genres** that Susan Rowland calls "aesthetic science" and that Jung himself would likely call "visionary literature" (using his own critical category, applied to himself despite his protests).

### The Ratio Shifts by Text

| Text | Poetic Quotient | Scholarly Quotient | Notes |
|------|-----------------|--------------------|-------|
| *The Red Book* | 90% | 10% | Calligraphic, illuminated, prophetic; "Jung without his conceptual arsenal" |
| *Septem Sermones ad Mortuos* (1916) | 95% | 5% | Written in the voice of Basilides, pure gnostic poetry |
| *Answer to Job* | 65% | 35% | Passionate, wrathful, theologically provocative tone |
| *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* | 50% | 50% | Oral-poetic narrative intertwined with conceptual reflection |
| *The Secret of the Golden Flower* Commentary | 40% | 60% | Strikingly lucid, appreciative, cross-cultural poetic resonance |
| *Psychology and Alchemy* | 30% | 70% | Vast scholarly apparatus, but the alchemical images pull toward the poetic |
| *Psychological Types* (1921) | 15% | 85% | Most scholastically dense of Jung's major works; the Definitions chapter is pure taxonomy |
| *Aion* (1951) | 20% | 80% | The Christ/Antichrist sections read as theological poetry embedded in historical scholarship |
| *Mysterium Coniunctionis* (1955) | 25% | 75% | Late, dense, scholarly summit -- yet the climax reaches for something beyond scholarship |
| *Synchronicity* essay (1952) | 35% | 65% | A scientific essay that attempts to bring Eros (meaning, connection) into the Logos of physics |
| *Man and His Symbols* (1961) | 30% | 70% | Deliberately accessible, written in English, for general readers |
| *Tavistock Lectures* (1935) | 10% | 90% | Conversational, explanatory, pedagogical |

### Where the Poetry Leaks Through

Even in his most scholarly mode, Jung's prose is punctuated by moments of **visionary compression** -- aphoristic sentences that condense an entire argument into a few words:

> "One does not become enlightened by imagining figures of light, but by making the darkness conscious." -- *CW 13, Alchemical Studies*

> "The shoe that fits one person pinches another; there is no recipe for living that suits all cases." -- *Modern Man in Search of a Soul* (1933)

> "The pendulum of the mind oscillates between sense and nonsense, not between right and wrong." -- *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*

These aphorisms are not parenthetical ornaments. They are **structural climaxes** -- the point in the argument where the dense scholarly reasoning collapses into a single, luminous image. Jung writes *toward* these moments.

### What Pure Scholarly Writing Would Look Like (and Why Jung Doesn't Do It)

A pure scientific psychologist would:
- State the hypothesis
- Describe the method
- Report the data
- Draw statistically supported conclusions
- Acknowledge limitations
- Cite only peer-reviewed sources

Jung instead:
- Begins with an image, a myth, a patient's dream, or an alchemical text
- Amplifies it by cross-referencing parallel material from other epochs and cultures
- Identifies the "archetypal pattern" that unifies the disparate instances
- Draws a conclusion that is often **amplificatory** rather than deductive
- Treats clinical experience, mythology, alchemy, ethnography, and personal introspection as **equally valid evidence**
- The "evidence" is the **convergence of independent sources** on the same pattern, rather than statistical correlation

This makes him frustrating to empiricists and invigorating to humanists.

**Source**: Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (Routledge, 2005); Rowland, "Jung, the Trickster Writer" (*Journal of Analytical Psychology*, 2006); Mathew V. Spano, "Modern(-ist) Man in Search of a Soul" (*Jung Page*, 2012).

## 9. THE RED BOOK: The Jungian Ur-Text

### "Jung Without His Conceptual Arsenal" (Shamdasani)

*The Red Book* (1914-1930, published 2009) is the Rosetta Stone of Jung's expression DNA. It is his most personal, least constrained text -- a handwritten, illuminated folio in which he recorded and elaborated his "confrontation with the unconscious" from 1913 to 1917, and refined for publication until 1930.

### Three Distinct Registers

Mathew V. Spano identifies **three distinct voices** operating in *The Red Book*:

1. **The Descriptive/Reporter voice** -- narrating events, visions, dialogues: "I was walking along a country road..."
2. **The Conceptual/Analytical voice** -- stepping back to comment: "What this vision means is..."
3. **The Mantic/Prophetic voice** -- delivering oracular pronouncements in heightened, biblical cadences: "The spirit of the depths opened my eyes and I saw..."

These registers shift **without warning**, creating the disorienting, Modernist texture that Spano compares to Hesse's *Steppenwolf* and Joyce's *Ulysses*.

### Language at Its Limit

A fundamental tension runs through *The Red Book* about the adequacy of language itself:

> "My speech is imperfect. Not because I want to shine with words, but out of the impossibility of finding those words. I speak in images. With nothing else can I express the words from the depth."

And yet in another passage:

> "Have you no Gods anymore?" "No, words are all we have..."

This oscillation -- between the impossibility of adequate language and the necessity of words as the only available gods -- is the generative tension of the entire text.

### The "Decorum of Imperfection"

Randy Fertel argues that *The Red Book* adopts a **"decorum of imperfection"** -- an aesthetic of roughness that serves to authenticate the experience as arising from a source beyond the personal ego. The text's very ungainliness and unfinished quality is part of its rhetorical strategy: "This is not a well-written book," Shamdasani notes, "but it is more effective precisely for that reason. It jars with his own category of the aesthetic."

### Genre Ambiguity

Scholars debate what *The Red Book* actually is:
- A personal diary/journal (Kirsch & Henderson)
- A work of psychology in literary form (Shamdasani)
- Modernist visionary literature comparable to Dante or Dostoyevsky (Spano)
- A "new gospel" in the prophetic tradition (Fertel)
- An improvisation in the tradition of automatic writing and Surrealism

The text itself **refuses genre classification** -- which is, of course, exactly the point.

### The "My Friends" Address

A crucial detail: Jung added the vocative address **"my friends"** to *The Red Book* -- a feature absent from the original Black Books. This signals conscious **literary construction aimed at an audience**, not private journaling. The entire text was retrofitted for publication, even though its "spontaneous" quality was carefully preserved as an *achieved effect*.

**Source**: Sonu Shamdasani, Introduction and editorial apparatus to *The Red Book: Liber Novus* (Norton, 2009); Mathew V. Spano, "Modern(-ist) Man in Search of a Soul: Jung's Red Book as Modernist Visionary Literature" (*Jung Page*, 2012); Randy Fertel, *A Taste for Chaos: The Art of Literary Improvisation* (Spring Journal Books, 2015); Sanford L. Drob, "The Red Book on Language" (*E-Jungian*, 2015).

## 10. MEMORIES, DREAMS, REFLECTIONS: The Late Voice

### Not a Conventional Autobiography

Jung was deeply skeptical of autobiographies, calling them full of "self-deceptions and downright lies." *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* is a deliberate **anti-autobiography** -- a record of inner experience, not external biography. His wife and children are scarcely mentioned. Dates, travels, and professional milestones are omitted or glossed over. As Aniela Jaffe writes in her introduction:

> "The chapters are rapidly moving beams of light that only fleetingly illuminate the outward events of Jung's life and work. In recompense, they transmit the atmosphere of his intellectual world and the experience of a man to whom the psyche was a profound reality."

### The Oral-Mediated Voice

The book's prose has the **cadence of spoken conversation** because much of it originated in Jung's weekly oral narrations to Aniela Jaffe, who transcribed his words. Jaffe explains:

> "Conversation or spontaneous narration is inevitably casual, and the tone has carried over into the entire 'autobiography.'"

This creates a distinctive hybrid style:
- Warm, personal, reflective
- Less dense than the *Collected Works* but still conceptually rich
- Anecdotal rather than systematic
- The voice of an 83-year-old man looking back on a long life, telling stories

### The "Personal Myth" Frame

The organizing principle is Jung's concept of **"personal myth"** -- the idea that a life story is best told not through facts but through the mythic patterns that shaped inner development. He asks:

> "But then what is your myth -- the myth in which you do live?"

The autobiography enacts this principle, treating his life as a narrative of the Self's realization through the unconscious. Susan Rowland argues that *Memories* transitions from linear life-story to a **non-linear, mythical mode** organized by repetition, ritual, dream, and metaphor.

### Structure: Spiral, Not Linear

The book moves through life stages but in a **thematic/spiral** pattern rather than strict chronology. Chapters circle around core insights (childhood, the Tower, the confrontation with the unconscious, travels, late thoughts) rather than proceeding year by year. The structure is itself mandala-like -- a recurring pattern in Jung's own life and work.

### Fusion of Voices

*Memories, Dreams, Reflections* is a **composite text**:
- Parts written entirely by Jung in longhand (especially the childhood chapters and "Late Thoughts")
- Parts transcribed from conversation by Jaffe
- Parts adapted from his 1925 seminar
- Jaffe's editing hand shaping the whole

This hybrid authorship makes it neither pure autobiography nor pure biography. Jaffe described the process: "the closer became the fusion between his work and mine." The result is what scholars call an "auto/biography."

### The Late Thoughts: Dense, Philosophical, Terminal

The final chapter, "Late Thoughts," is the book's most philosophically dense section. Here the prose tightens, the protective warmth of the storytelling voice recedes, and Jung offers some of his most compressed metaphysical speculations. It reads almost like a **last testament** -- not casual, not conversational, but the distilled essence of a lifetime's contemplation.

### Jung's Refusal to Include It in the Collected Works

Jung insisted that *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* NOT be included in his *Collected Works*. He considered it a story, not science -- a "personal myth," not psychology. This distinction is itself a crucial fact about his expression DNA: he was acutely conscious of **genre boundaries** and deployed different voices for different purposes.

**Source**: C.G. Jung, *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (1961), recorded and edited by Aniela Jaffe, translated by Richard and Clara Winston; Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (Routledge, 2005); reviews from *The Atlantic* (2012) and SuperSummary.

## 11. THE PERFORMATIVE PRINCIPLE: Writing as Enactment

### The Core Thesis of Rowland's Analysis

Susan Rowland's single most important contribution to understanding Jung's style is the argument that Jung's writing does not merely **describe** the psyche's creativity -- it **enacts** and **performs** it. His famously circular, digressive prose is not a flaw but the *point*:

> "Jung's writing holds the tension between the desire to know something definitely and completely and the requirement to keep the creativity of the psyche as part of the act of cognition." -- Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer*

### The Trickster Writer

Rowland calls Jung a **"trickster writer"** -- a boundary-crosser whose prose refuses the either/or logic of Enlightenment modernity. His texts:
- Cross genres (science/art, clinical/creative, scholarly/visionary)
- Resist linear argumentation in favor of circular, spiraling amplification
- Allow the "irrational" unconscious to participate in meaning-making at the level of form
- Use myth, metaphor, and symbol as **constitutive** elements of argument, not decorative flourishes

### Specific Genre Innovations

Rowland identifies several genre-bending achievements:
- **"On the Nature of the Psyche"**: a cross between a scientific essay and science fiction
- **Answer to Job**: an autobiographical form for the "Jungian self as spatiality"
- **Aion**: a "novel in the Bakhtinian sense" (dialogical, polyphonic)

### Curative Writing

Rowland presents Jung as a **curative writer** for the ills of modernity -- his project being to heal the wounded psyche through language itself. The "aesthetic science" is not a compromise between art and science but a deliberate third form: one that reintroduces the excluded feminine (Eros, meaning, connection, image) into the masculine Logos-dominated structure of modern rational discourse.

### Bakhtin and the Dialogical Jung

Rowland connects Jung's concept of the psyche with Mikhail Bakhtin's "dialogical imagination." Both thinkers recognized a cultural and psychic tension between centripetal forces (which unify, homogenize, impose one meaning) and centrifugal forces (which diversify, pluralize, produce many meanings). Jung's prose -- with its multiple voices, its refusal of single conclusions, its circling around a problem without "solving" it -- is a **dialogical text**, enacting the psyche's polyphonic nature.

**Source**: Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (Routledge, 2005); Rowland, "Jung, the Trickster Writer, or What Literary Research Can Do for the Clinician" (*Journal of Analytical Psychology*, April 2006); Routlegde catalog description for *Jung as a Writer*.

## 12. COMPOSITIONAL PROCESS: Active Imagination as Generative Method

### How Jung Wrote

Jung's compositional method was inseparable from his psychological method. The core practice was **active imagination** -- a deliberate technique for engaging the unconscious through vision, dialogue, and art. This was not merely a therapeutic intervention for patients; it was Jung's own **compositional engine**.

### The Three-Phase Process

| Phase | Description | Output |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **1. Black Books (1913-1914)** | Spontaneous daily notes: dreams, visions, imaginative dialogues. Written in real-time heat, with automatic/dictation qualities. | Six volumes of black-bound notebooks |
| **2. Typed Manuscript** | Transcribed, expanded with commentary, associations, further reflection. | Typed draft |
| **3. The Red Book (1914-1930)** | Calligraphically transcribed and illuminated with paintings. Raw material transformed into crafted work for a public audience. | One folio volume, 205 pages |

### The Paradox of Spontaneous Craft

The Red Book's rhetoric emphasizes spontaneity -- the material "forces itself upon the author," "his hand is seized, his pen writes things that his mind contemplates with amazement." Yet the text was reworked for **over 15 years**. The improvisational authenticity is an **achieved effect**, not raw dictation.

### The Decorum of Imperfection Revisited

Fertel traces a long counter-tradition of improvisational authenticity, from prophetic texts to Surrealist automatic writing to stream-of-consciousness novels. Jung's "decorum of imperfection" places him in this tradition: the roughness of the text *authenticates* it as coming from a source deeper than the personal ego.

### The Unconscious Writes Through Form

Jung's mature compositions follow a recognizable rhythm:
1. Start with a specific, concrete phenomenon (a patient's dream, a myth, an alchemical image)
2. Amplify by gathering parallels from diverse sources (mythology, religion, ethnography, literature)
3. Allow the pattern to emerge through **convergence** of independent testimonies
4. Draw a conclusion that is as much **experienced** as it is logically deduced
5. The writing *enacts* the conclusion -- the reader experiences the pattern emerging, not just being told about it

**Source**: C.G. Jung, *The Red Book: Liber Novus* (Norton, 2009); Randy Fertel, "Jung's Red Book, Improvisation, and the Metis Spirit" (*International Journal of Jungian Studies*, 2017); Wikipedia, "Active Imagination" entry; *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*, Chapter VI, "Confrontation with the Unconscious."

## 13. WRITING IN ENGLISH: The Late-Language Shift

### Man and His Symbols: Written Directly in English

Jung's final book, *Man and His Symbols* (1964, posthumous), was **written directly in English** rather than German. This was a deliberate choice to reach the broadest possible audience after the success of the 1959 BBC *Face to Face* interview.

Key features of Jung's English prose:
- **Simpler syntax** than his German academic writing
- **Shorter sentences**, edited for general-reader intelligibility after his death
- **Direct address** to the reader ("you"), which is rare in the *Collected Works*
- **Abundant illustrations** to ground abstract concepts
- A **collaborative structure** (four co-authors) that distributes the explanatory burden

### The Editorial Hand

Jung's English chapter underwent "fairly extensive editing to improve its intelligibility to the general reader" after his death. This means the late English prose is **mediated** -- closer to Jung's *intention for accessibility* than to his natural written voice.

### The BBC Interview: Jung Speaking English

In the 1959 BBC interview, Jung speaks English with a strong Swiss-German accent but with remarkable fluency. His spoken English reveals:
- Hesitation followed by carefully constructed aphoristic statements
- A tendency to use simple, declarative sentences in conversation
- The same gift for **paradoxical compression** evident in his best writing
- A **warm, grandfatherly** tone in spoken delivery that differs markedly from the dense scholarly voice of the *Collected Works*

**Source**: BBC *Face to Face* interview (October 22, 1959); Wikipedia, "Man and His Symbols" article; correspondence regarding the book's editing process.

## 14. CHARACTERISTIC RHETORICAL PHRASES AND STRUCTURAL TICS

Based on analysis of the *Collected Works* and scholarly commentary, Jung has several distinctive rhetorical habits:

### Opening Gambits
- **"It is a well-known fact that..."** -- even when the "fact" is highly esoteric
- **"As experience shows..."** (wie die Erfahrung zeigt) -- his most common evidence-appeal
- **"We may therefore venture to..."** -- the scholar-as-explorer framing
- **"The reader may object that..."** -- preemptive concession

### The Amplification Pattern
Jung's most characteristic structural move: present a dream/myth/image, then amplify it through a cascade of:
1. A parallel from alchemy
2. A parallel from Gnosticism
3. A parallel from a patient's case
4. A parallel from ethnography
5. The conclusion that "this is the archetype of X"

### Self-Referential Circling
- **"As I have already pointed out..."** (wie ich bereits dargelegt habe)
- **"We have already seen..."**
- **"This brings us back to..."**
- **"Once more, I should like to emphasize..."**

These phrases build the circular, spiral structure that Rowland identifies as performative of Jung's psychology.

### The Definition Mode
In *Psychological Types* and elsewhere, Jung adopts a distinctive **lexicographical voice** -- defining terms with the precision of a dictionary entry but the depth of a philosophical essay. The "Definitions" chapter of *Psychological Types* runs to over 100 pages and operates as a self-contained glossary of his conceptual world.

### The "So to Speak" (Sozusagen) Hedge
Perhaps Jung's most frequent rhetorical tic. Functions to signal metaphorical usage while preserving the ontological weight of what is being claimed.

**Source**: Analysis drawn from C.G. Jung, *Collected Works* (20 volumes), especially *CW 6 (Psychological Types)*, *CW 7 (Two Essays)*, *CW 9i (Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious)*; Susan Rowland, *Jung as a Writer* (2005).

## 15. SUMMARY: THE JUNGIAN EXPRESSION DNA CHART

| Dimension | Characteristic |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Sentence architecture** | Long, subordinate-rich, Germanic hypotaxis in academic works; shorter, conversational in seminars; fragmented/oracular in *Red Book* |
| **Analogy density** | Very high. Alchemy, geology, water, light, energy, architecture, botany are primary domains |
| **Metaphor use** | Constitutive (argument is done through metaphor), not ornamental |
| **Private vocabulary** | ~15 proprietary terms, each with rigorous definitions; absent from *Red Book* where figures embody the concepts |
| **Uncertainty expression** | Rarely "I don't know"; prefers redescribing the unknown as *mysterium*, using anticipatory concession, "so to speak" hedging, "experience shows" appeals, embedded admission, and the "I know" *gnosis* claim |
| **Humor type** | Dry, earthy, self-deprecating, paradox-based; deployed to undercut pretension and dogmatism |
| **Citation habits** | Polyphonic mosaic. Goethe most-cited literary source; alchemical texts vast; Bible central; Nietzsche; Kant/Schopenhauer; Gnostic texts; *I Ching*; Chinese/Daoist texts |
| **Paradox handling** | Sustains contradiction without resolution; transcendent function produces *tertium non datur* from *coincidentia oppositorum* |
| **Poetic/scholarly ratio** | Varies from 90% poetic (*Red Book*) to 85% scholarly (*Psychological Types*); aphorisms serve as structural climaxes in otherwise scholarly texts |
| **Genre identity** | Deliberately hybrid -- "aesthetic science," "visionary literature," "trickster writing" -- refuses either/or of art vs. science |
| **Compositional method** | Active imagination engenders raw material; extensive revision produces achieved spontaneity ("decorum of imperfection") |
| **Late voice** | Accessible, oral-conversational, mythic-narrative, composite authorship (Jung + Jaffe), refuses inclusion in *Collected Works* |
| **Performative principle** | Writing *enacts* what it describes -- the psyche's polyphonic creativity is performed at the level of form |
| **Most characteristic phrase structure** | "X is... but... and yet..." -- qualification nested inside assertion, circling toward insight |
